The Free Lunch:

EB-5 Capital Gerrymandering of Targeted Employment Areas

by Wuyahuang Li, Alberto Meouchi & Olivia Serra, October 29, 2020



Costing a total of $25 billion, Hudson Yard is the most expensive real-estate development project in US history. The primary developer of the 26-acre West Side Yards in midtown Manhattan is Related Companies, one of the largest developers in the US, along with Oxford Property Groups. In addition to the investment capital of these developers, Hudson Yard collects $1.6 billion from the fifth employment-based (EB-5) visa program, the Golden Visa [1] providing a fast track to trade US residency and citizenship with wealthy foreign investors for investment capital. As stated by public records and analysis by Matthew Haag at the New School, this financial capital is covered by a $6 billion “tax break” package from the federal government, double what was granted to incentivize the new Amazon campus in Queens.

To understand the context of financial gerrymandering, we have to go back to 1990, when the U.S. Congress created the fifth employment-based preference immigrant visa for foreign nationals who may create at least 10 U.S. jobs by funding commercial entities. Its purposes are not only to stimulate the U.S. economy through the creation and capital investment but to raise funding to invest in underdeveloped rural regions or high unemployment areas in the metropolis. To be designated as the Targeted Employment Area (TEA), an area bound by county census tracts must have an unemployment rate over 1.5 times the national average.

The practice of readlining targeted employment area is called gerrymandering, first by Kriston Capps at CityLab, now adopted by US Citizenship and Immigration Services. According to american community survey data, Hudson Yards’ unemployment rate in 2014 is 5.0, lower than national average 6.6%. Through gerrymandering, it reaches 12.6%, qualifying itself as a highly unemployment area.

Hudson Yards successfully subverts the EB- 5’s founding principles to entice investment in poverty-stricken areas to the extreme extent that it strips one year’s worth of EB-5 capital off the entire country.



A continuous set of American Community Surveys proves that four out five residential tracts in Harlem remain highly unemployed, ranging from 16.8% to 21.1% in 2017 and fourfold the national average. Amongst the Hispanic community in the gerrymandered tracts, DeWitt Clinton Houses is a non- continuous campus of 6 buildings, covering an area of 5.6 acres. The program has 749 apartment units and houses 1,823 people. One out of every five are unemployed.



In 2014, tenants of DeWitt Clinton Houses filed a lawsuit against NYCHA for overlooking maintenance and public health hazards. The public housing agency claims to be $35 billion underfunded and trades vacant lots with developer in exchange for inclusionary affordable housing.



Despite its purpose to serve rural or high employment areas, most of the capitals have flowed into the development of luxurious condos and hotels. According to the Annual Report (2000-2018) provided by the U.S. State Department Visa Office, an average of 97% of capitals raised by 5th Employment Preference is allocated to metropolitan regions. From 2007 to nowadays, the number of regional centers grew from merely 16 to 865, with locations operating across 50 states in US.

Accordingly, areas with high levels of development and immigration boast the densest concentration of regional centers. Once peripheral, EB-5 has since emerged as a mainstream financing option in these places and beyond. At the moment, only NYU’s Stern School of Business has center dedicated to the EB-5 Capital Project Database.



A renewed EB-5 regulation came into place on November 21, 2019, and it informed a positive change on EB-5 capital gerrymandering. Under the COVID-19 pandemic, unemployment rate surges nationwide, hitting racially segregated urban zones differently. Will the practice EB-5 gerrymandering have its comeback with the call from the distressed financial market?
